Can you be an abolitionist and campaign for a reform? Yes — if the reform is non-reformist. Ending the imprisonment of pregnant women is exactly that kind of reform.

## What is a non-reformist reform?

The concept comes from the abolitionist tradition. A non-reformist reform is one that reduces the power and reach of carceral systems rather than expanding or legitimising them. It shrinks the footprint of incarceration. It redirects resources toward communities. And it opens space to imagine further transformation.

Ending pregnancy imprisonment does all of these things.

## Why this isn't just about pregnant women

When we argue that no pregnant woman should be imprisoned, we are not arguing for a special category of deserving prisoner. We are arguing that imprisonment is incompatible with bodily safety, with healthcare, and with the wellbeing of children — and that these incompatibilities are most visibly catastrophic when the person imprisoned is pregnant.

The deaths of babies in women's prisons in the UK are not exceptional failures. They are the predictable result of a system that was never designed to accommodate the humanity of the people it holds.

## What we're demanding

Level Up's Pregnancy in Prison campaign calls for an end to custodial sentences for pregnant women and new mothers. Not better conditions in prison. Not more medical support in prison. An end to their imprisonment.

This is a realistic demand. Other countries have moved in this direction. The UK's own sentencing guidelines have shifted. The direction of travel is clear.

## The abolitionist horizon

We hold this campaign alongside a broader vision: a world in which the carceral system does not exist in its current form, and in which communities have the resources they need to keep people safe without prisons. Ending pregnancy imprisonment is one step on that road.
