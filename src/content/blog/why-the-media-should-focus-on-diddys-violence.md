When Sean Combs was arrested, the headlines were full of baby oil and "freak-offs". The prurience was almost total. What got far less coverage was the pattern of coercive control, physical violence, and abuse of power that survivors had described for years.

## Why framing matters

When the media covers a case like this as a story about sexual excess rather than violence and coercion, it does several things. It titillates rather than informs. It obscures the systemic nature of the abuse. And it implicitly frames victims as participants in transgression rather than targets of harm.

This is not new. It's the same framing we see in domestic homicide coverage — where a man's violence is filtered through his passion, his mental state, his exceptional circumstances. The woman who died is rendered secondary to the story of how he felt.

## What accountability journalism looks like

Accountability journalism about powerful men who commit sexual violence focuses on the pattern of behaviour, the use of power and resources to silence victims, the institutional failures that allowed abuse to continue, and the experiences of survivors.

It does not focus on the details of sexual acts, the lifestyle of the perpetrator, or the question of whether victims "really" objected.

## Our media guidelines

This is exactly why Level Up produced the UK's first media guidelines on reporting domestic abuse deaths — and why we continue to push for better standards. The way we tell these stories shapes what people understand about violence, who gets believed, and who is held accountable.

The Diddy coverage was a missed opportunity. It didn't have to be.
