There is a line that runs through a lot of feminist advocacy. On one side: the sympathetic victim — innocent, blameless, easy to defend. On the other: the woman whose history, choices, or identity make her harder to campaign for.

This is the line of deservingness. And it is making our feminism smaller.

## Who gets left out

When we campaign for domestic abuse survivors, we often lead with the most "innocent" cases. When we talk about sex workers, we prioritise those who are trafficked over those who chose their work. When we discuss women in prison, we highlight the wrongly convicted rather than the guilty.

This is understandable as a communications strategy. But as a political framework, it is dangerous. It accepts the premise that some women deserve protection and others don't. It lets us off the hook from asking harder questions about the systems that harm everyone.

## Solidarity means everyone

Level Up's campaigns try to resist this. Our Pregnancy in Prison campaign doesn't only ask: is this woman innocent? It asks: should any pregnant woman be in prison? Our Abortion is Healthcare campaign doesn't only defend "good" abortions. It defends all of them.

This is what it means to centre bodily autonomy rather than respectability.

## The world we're building

We believe that feminism's most powerful offer is liberation — not just for the women who are easy to love, but for everyone. That means confronting the ways our own movements can reproduce the hierarchies we claim to oppose.

None of us are free until all of us are free. That's not a slogan. It's a framework.
